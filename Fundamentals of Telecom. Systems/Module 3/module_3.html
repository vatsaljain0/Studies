Telecommunication Networks

Detailed Study Guide: Network Types, Satellites, Topologies, and Deep Switching Mechanisms

1. Terms Related to Telecommunication Technology

A telecommunication network is a collection of nodes and links that enables communication between devices. To make these networks functional, several specific pieces of hardware are utilized:

  • Modem: A device that allows computers to connect to the internet or communication networks. It modulates and demodulates signals to transmit and receive data.
  • Router: A device that connects computers and devices to the internet or other networks. It routes data packets between devices and networks and can also serve as a firewall.
  • Switch: A device that allows computers and devices to communicate with each other on the same network. It receives data packets and sends them intelligently to their intended destination.
  • Server: A computer that stores and manages data and applications, providing them to other computers over a network. Used to host websites and process data.
  • Cloud: A network of servers connected over the internet used to store, process, and manage data and applications remotely. It allows users to access data from anywhere.

2. The PSTN (Public Switched Telephone Network)

The PSTN is primarily used for traditional voice communication, enabling landline calls for households and businesses. It supports services like fax transmissions and is often used for emergency services due to its reliability.

Block Diagram: Architecture of the PSTN

Core Components of PSTN

  • Subscriber Lines: These connect individual telephones or devices to the PSTN. These copper or fiber-optic lines link users to central offices, allowing access to the larger network. They serve as the first point of entry into the PSTN.
  • Line Signaling: The process of transmitting information to manage the initiation, progress, and termination of a call. It handles tasks like detecting when a handset is lifted (off-hook) and recognizing dialed numbers.
  • Trunk Lines: High-capacity connections linking telephone exchanges. Unlike subscriber lines serving individuals, trunk lines handle aggregated traffic from multiple calls, allowing efficient long-distance transmission between different regions.

Working: What happens when a phone number is dialed?

When a phone number is dialed, the system initiates a series of actions:

  1. The local exchange captures the dialed number and determines the route—whether it’s within the local area or requires connection to remote exchanges.
  2. The network uses switching systems to establish a dedicated, physical circuit for the call.
  3. Once connected, real-time communication begins, which remains uninterrupted until the call ends.
  4. When the call ends, the circuit is released.

Processing of Toll Calls

Toll calls (long-distance calls requiring extra charges) are routed through regional and national exchanges. The system calculates tariffs based on the distance and duration of the call. The call is then connected using trunk lines and higher-tier exchanges, ensuring accurate billing and high call quality.

How does PSTN differ from VoIP?

PSTN and VoIP differ fundamentally in technology and infrastructure.

  • Technology: PSTN relies on circuit-switched networks (creating a fixed pathway). VoIP (Voice over Internet Protocol) uses packet-switched networks (transmitting voice data over the internet).
  • Pros/Cons: PSTN is known for high reliability and call quality but has higher operational costs. VoIP is flexible, cost-effective, and uses existing internet connections, but depends entirely on reliable internet access.

3. Cellular and Satellite Networks

A. Cellular Networks: Architecture

A cellular network divides a geographic area into smaller blocks called "cells", allowing the reuse of radio frequencies to support a massive number of users.

  • Mobile Equipment (ME): Refers to the physical phone itself. Uniquely identified by the IMEI number.
  • What is a Cell: A base station (transmitter) having a number of RF channels. A Cell Radius is approx. 30 Km at startup, and 1 KM when mature.
  • Base Transceiver Station (BTS): The antenna installed on top of a tower. It is the phone's access point to the network. It handles speech encoding and modulation of radio signals.
  • Base Station Controller (BSC): Controls multiple BTSs. It handles the allocation of radio channels and handovers between its BTSs.
  • Mobile Switching Center (MSC) / MTSO: The heart of the network. It handles call routing, call setup, coordinates handoffs between different BSCs, and interfaces with the PSTN.

B. Satellite Communication

The Need for Satellite Communication

Earlier, Ground wave and Sky wave propagation were used, but they were strictly limited to distances of about 1500 KM due to the earth's curvature. Satellite communication overcomes this limitation by acting as a microwave repeater station in space, bouncing signals over oceans and continents well beyond the line of sight.

How it works: The satellite acts as a Transponder. It receives a signal from Earth on the Uplink Frequency, amplifies it, translates it to a completely different Downlink Frequency (to avoid the signals colliding), and blasts it back down to a different Earth station.

Types of Satellites (Based on Orbit)

Satellites are classified by how high they orbit the Earth. The higher they are, the more area they cover, but the slower the connection becomes.

  • 1. GEO (Geostationary Earth Orbit)

    Distance: ~36,000 km above Earth.
    How it works: At this massive height, the satellite takes exactly 24 hours to orbit the Earth. This means it moves at the exact same speed the Earth spins. To us on the ground, the satellite appears to be completely frozen in one spot in the sky.
    Analogy: Like attaching a camera to a long pole on a spinning globe; it always looks at the exact same city.
    Use Case: Satellite TV (Direct-to-Home) and weather monitoring. You point your dish at it once, and never have to move it. (Drawback: Very high latency/delay because of the huge distance).

  • 2. MEO (Medium Earth Orbit)

    Distance: Between 2,000 km and 36,000 km.
    How it works: They orbit faster than the Earth spins, usually circling the globe twice a day. You need a constellation (group) of about 24 MEO satellites to cover the entire planet continuously.
    Use Case: The absolute backbone of GPS (Global Positioning System) and navigation. It provides a perfect balance between good coverage area and acceptable signal delay.

  • 3. LEO (Low Earth Orbit)

    Distance: Under 2,000 km (Very close to Earth).
    How it works: Because they are so close, the signal travels instantly (extremely low latency). However, they zip across the sky very fast (orbiting in just 90 minutes). A single satellite is only visible to you for a few minutes before it vanishes over the horizon.
    Analogy: Imagine a massive swarm of fast bees flying just above your head. As one bee flies past you, it hands your signal off to the next bee flying behind it.
    Use Case: High-speed Satellite Internet (like Elon Musk's Starlink) and satellite phones. Requires thousands of satellites to form a continuous grid.

4. Network Topologies (Detailed)

A Network Topology refers to the physical or logical layout of how nodes (computers, printers, routers) are wired or connected to each other in a network.

A. Star Topology

In a Star topology, every single device in the network connects directly to a central piece of hardware, usually a Hub or a Switch. Devices never connect directly to each other. If Computer A wants to talk to Computer B, it must send the data to the central Hub, which then forwards it to Computer B.

Advantages:

  • Extremely easy to install, manage, and add new computers without disrupting the network.
  • Robust: If one cable snaps, only that specific computer goes offline. The rest of the network survives.

Disadvantages:

  • The central Hub is a single point of failure. If the Hub loses power or breaks, the entire network collapses instantly.

B. Mesh Topology

In a fully connected Mesh topology, every single node has a dedicated, physical point-to-point cable connecting it to every other node in the network. For 'n' devices, you need exactly n(n-1)/2 physical cables. Data travels on its own private highway between any two devices.

Advantages:

  • Ultimate reliability. If one cable breaks, data simply routes through another path. Highly secure as data isn't shared on a central hub.

Disadvantages:

  • Extremely expensive and practically impossible to wire in large networks due to the insane number of cables required.

C. Ring Topology

Computers are connected in a closed-loop circle. Each node connects exactly to its left and right neighbor. Data travels in a single direction. To prevent data collisions, the network uses a digital "Token." A computer can only transmit data when it possesses the Token.

Advantages:

  • Very organized. Performs excellently under heavy traffic loads because the token system prevents data collisions entirely.

Disadvantages:

  • A single cable break or a single dead computer breaks the entire ring, paralyzing the whole network.

D. Bus Topology

Every computer and device is connected to one single, long main cable called the "backbone." Devices connect to this backbone using drop lines and taps. When a computer sends a message, it blasts it down the entire length of the cable in both directions.

Advantages:

  • Extremely cost-effective as it requires the absolute least amount of cabling. Easy to add a node.

Disadvantages:

  • If the main backbone cable fails, everything dies. High chance of data collisions if many computers talk at once.

E. Tree Topology

It organizes the network in a hierarchy, combining the features of Bus and Star topologies. It has a central "Root" node at the top. Secondary nodes branch off the root, and tertiary nodes branch off those. It must have at least 3 levels of depth to be a true tree.

Advantages:

  • Highly scalable for massive organizations (like connecting different departments across a campus). Easy fault identification.

Disadvantages:

  • If a parent node (like a department router) fails, the entire branch of computers below it gets disconnected.

F. Hybrid Topology

A Hybrid topology is simply a combination of two or more different standard topologies, resulting in a unique structure. For example, connecting a Star network in the IT department to a Bus network in the Sales department creates a Star-Bus Hybrid.

Advantages:

  • Extremely flexible and highly reliable, tailored to exactly what the business needs.

Disadvantages:

  • By far the most expensive and complex topology to design, install, and troubleshoot due to mixed hardware.

5. Masterclass: Switching and Routing

In large networks, there isn't just one single wire connecting you to your friend. The internet is a massive web of intersections. Switching is the mechanism of directing data traffic at these intersections, ensuring your data travels from the input port to the correct output port.

A. The 3 Types of Switching Networks

1. Circuit-Switched Networks

Before data can be sent, the network physically locks down a dedicated path of wires and switches all the way from the sender to the receiver. This path belongs ONLY to them until they hang up. It involves 3 strict phases: Setup, Data Transfer, and Teardown.

Analogy: Imagine paying the police to block off an entire highway lane from your house to your friend's house so only your car can drive on it.

Pros & Cons: Perfect for voice calls (PSTN) because once the path is built, there is zero delay. But it is terribly inefficient for computer data; if you pause typing, the entire dedicated wire sits empty and wasted.

2. Message-Switched Networks

An older technique that uses a "Store and Forward" mechanism. The entire, complete message is sent to a switching node. The node saves the whole message to its hard drive, waits until the next path is clear, and then forwards the entire message to the next node.

Analogy: The traditional postal mail system. You give a whole box to the post office. They store it in a warehouse until a truck is ready, then drive it to the next post office.

Pros & Cons: Great for preventing traffic jams, but useless for real-time video/voice because the delay of storing and forwarding massive files is way too long.

3. Packet-Switched Networks (The Modern Internet)

Instead of sending one massive file, the data is chopped up into thousands of tiny, equal-sized pieces called Packets. Paths are shared by millions of users simultaneously. It comes in two flavors:

  • Datagram Networks: Every single packet is an independent traveler. It has the destination address stamped on it, and routers decide on the fly which path is fastest. Packets might take completely different routes and arrive out of order! (Analogy: Tearing a book into single pages, putting them in envelopes, and mailing them separately. They arrive out of order, and the receiver pieces them back together).
  • Virtual-Circuit Networks: A hybrid! It chops data into packets, but it forces all packets to follow the exact same pre-planned path using a Virtual Circuit Identifier (VCI).

B. Inside the Switch: Hardware Techniques

How does a switch physically move data from an input wire to an output wire? Engineers use two clever electronic designs.

1. Space-Division Switching

This involves physically connecting wires together inside the switch. The most common type is the Crossbar Switch. Imagine a grid of horizontal input wires and vertical output wires. At every intersection, there is an electronic micro-switch (crosspoint). To connect Input 2 to Output 4, the switch simply snaps the crosspoint closed at coordinates (2,4).

Limitation: If you have 1000 inputs and 1000 outputs, you need 1,000,000 physical crosspoints! To fix this, engineers use Multistage Switches, linking smaller crossbars together to save hardware.

2. Time-Division Switching

Instead of building a massive grid of wires, this uses Time-Division Multiplexing (TDM) and computer memory (RAM) inside the switch. The key technology is the Time-Slot Interchange (TSI).

Analogy: A bartender takes drinks from 4 customers in order (1, 2, 3, 4). He places the drinks on a counter (the RAM). He then serves the drinks to the waiters in a completely shuffled order (e.g., 3, 1, 4, 2) based on who needs what. By shuffling the time slots, the data is successfully routed without needing a massive wire grid!

Note: Modern telephone networks combine both! They use a Time-Space-Time (TST) Switch, which uses time-slot shuffling to organize the data, a space-division crossbar to physically move it, and another time-slot shuffle to deliver it perfectly.

C. Overview of Routing

While switching is the mechanical act of moving data from an input port to an output port inside a box, Routing is the massive, global intelligence behind choosing which specific path across the entire country the data should take.

  • The GPS of the Internet: Routers use complex math algorithms to evaluate paths based on distance (how many jumps or 'hops' it takes), speed (bandwidth), and current traffic jams (congestion) to find the absolute fastest route.
  • Routing Tables: In a Datagram Network (like the Internet), every router contains a dynamic database called a Routing Table. When a packet arrives, the router examines its Destination IP address, compares it against the table, and forwards it to the next best router.
  • Dynamic Updates: Because the internet changes constantly (undersea cables snap, massive traffic spikes occur during the Superbowl), routers constantly gossip with each other using routing protocols (like OSPF or BGP) to update their tables dynamically and instantly reroute traffic around broken paths.
