Telecommunication Networks

Detailed Study Guide: Network Types, Topologies, and Working Mechanisms

1. Terms Related to Telecommunication Technology

A telecommunication network is a collection of nodes and links that enables communication between devices. To make these networks functional, several specific pieces of hardware are utilized:

  • Modem: A device that allows computers to connect to the internet or communication networks. It modulates and demodulates signals to transmit and receive data.
  • Router: A device that connects computers and devices to the internet or other networks. It routes data packets between devices and networks and can also serve as a firewall.
  • Switch: A device that allows computers and devices to communicate with each other on the same network. It receives data packets and sends them intelligently to their intended destination.
  • Server: A computer that stores and manages data and applications, providing them to other computers over a network. Used to host websites and process data.
  • Cloud: A network of servers connected over the internet used to store, process, and manage data and applications remotely. It allows users to access data from anywhere.

2. The PSTN (Public Switched Telephone Network)

The PSTN is primarily used for traditional voice communication, enabling landline calls for households and businesses. It supports services like fax transmissions and is often used for emergency services due to its reliability.

Block Diagram: Architecture of the PSTN

Core Components of PSTN

  • Subscriber Lines: These connect individual telephones or devices to the PSTN. These copper or fiber-optic lines link users to central offices, allowing access to the larger network. They serve as the first point of entry into the PSTN.
  • Line Signaling: The process of transmitting information to manage the initiation, progress, and termination of a call. It handles tasks like detecting when a handset is lifted (off-hook) and recognizing dialed numbers.
  • Trunk Lines: High-capacity connections linking telephone exchanges. Unlike subscriber lines serving individuals, trunk lines handle aggregated traffic from multiple calls, allowing efficient long-distance transmission between different regions.

Working: What happens when a phone number is dialed?

When a phone number is dialed, the system initiates a series of actions:

  1. The local exchange captures the dialed number and determines the route—whether it’s within the local area or requires connection to remote exchanges.
  2. The network uses switching systems to establish a dedicated, physical circuit for the call.
  3. Once connected, real-time communication begins, which remains uninterrupted until the call ends.
  4. When the call ends, the circuit is released.

Processing of Toll Calls

Toll calls (long-distance calls requiring extra charges) are routed through regional and national exchanges. The system calculates tariffs based on the distance and duration of the call. The call is then connected using trunk lines and higher-tier exchanges, ensuring accurate billing and high call quality.

How does PSTN differ from VoIP?

PSTN and VoIP differ fundamentally in technology and infrastructure.

  • Technology: PSTN relies on circuit-switched networks (creating a fixed pathway). VoIP (Voice over Internet Protocol) uses packet-switched networks (transmitting voice data over the internet).
  • Pros/Cons: PSTN is known for high reliability and call quality but has higher operational costs. VoIP is flexible, cost-effective, and uses existing internet connections, but depends entirely on reliable internet access.

3. Cellular and Satellite Networks

A. Cellular Networks: Components & Architecture

A cellular network divides a geographic area into smaller blocks called "cells", allowing the reuse of radio frequencies to support a massive number of users.

Cellular Network Architecture

Cellular Network Components

  • Mobile Equipment (ME): Refers to the physical phone itself. Older phones operated on a single band, while newer phones are dual-band, triple-band, or quad-band (capable of operating on networks worldwide). Each phone is uniquely identified by the IMEI (International Mobile Equipment Identity) number, burned into the phone by the manufacturer (usually found under the battery).
  • What is a Cell: A base station (transmitter) having a number of RF channels. Each cell covers a limited number of subscribers within its boundaries. A Cell Radius is approx. 30 Km at startup, and 1 KM when mature.
  • Cell Size and Capacity: Cell size determines the number of cells available to cover an area and the total capacity available to all users (with frequency reuse). Capacity is limited by bandwidth.
  • Base Transceiver Station (BTS): The antenna installed on top of a tower. It is the phone's access point to the network. It handles speech encoding, encryption, multiplexing, and modulation of radio signals. One BTS usually covers a 120-degree sector (a tower with 3 BTSs covers 360 degrees). A BTS is assigned a Cell Identity to denote its Location Area.
  • Base Station Controller (BSC): Controls multiple BTSs. It handles the allocation of radio channels, frequency administration, and handovers between its BTSs. It functions as a "funneler", reducing the number of connections going to the MSC.
  • Mobile Switching Center (MSC) / MTSO: The heart of the network. It handles call routing, call setup, and basic switching. An MSC handles multiple BSCs, coordinates inter-MSC handoffs, and interfaces with other networks (like PSTN).

Working of a Cellular Network (9-Step Process)

  1. When we turn on the cell phone, the Control Channel seeks to get the SID (System Identification Number).
  2. The Control Channel is a special frequency used by the phone and base station to communicate with each other.
  3. If the cell phone finds it difficult to get a connection to the control channel, a "no service" message is shown.
  4. If the SID is accessed, it compares it to the SID programmed on the phone. If they match, the phone recognizes it is communicating with its home system.
  5. The handset transmits a registration request. The MTSO keeps track of the location of your phone in a database so it knows which cell you are in when a call arrives.
  6. To receive a call, the MTSO searches its database to find your cell. The MTSO then chooses a frequency pair to take the call.
  7. The MTSO communicates over the control channel with the cell phone to tell it what frequencies to use. Once switched on, the call is associated.
  8. When the mobile phone moves towards the edge of the cell, the base station experiences a drop in signal power, while the base station in the cell the phone is traveling towards sees the signal intensity rise.
  9. By means of the MTSO, the two base stations coordinate. The cell phone gets a signal on a control channel guiding it to change frequencies. This executes the Handoff to the new cell.

B. Satellite Communication

The Need for Satellite Communication

Earlier, two kinds of propagation were used for communication over distances:

  • Ground wave propagation: Suitable for frequencies up to 30MHz. Makes use of the troposphere.
  • Sky wave propagation: Suitable for 30-40 MHz. Makes use of the ionosphere properties.

The Limitation: The maximum hop or station distance is limited to 1500 KM only in both ground and sky wave propagation due to the earth's curvature. Satellite communication overcomes this limitation, providing communication for long distances well beyond the line of sight.

Working Mechanism

A satellite is a body that moves around another body in a particular path. A communication satellite is essentially a microwave repeater station in space. It is helpful in telecommunications, radio, TV, and internet applications.

  • A normal repeater increases the strength of a received signal and transmits it. However, a satellite works as a Transponder. This means it changes the frequency band of the transmitted signal from the received one to avoid interference.
  • Uplink Frequency: The frequency with which the signal is sent from Earth into space.
  • Downlink Frequency: The frequency with which the signal is sent by the transponder back down to Earth.

4. Network Topologies

A Network Topology refers to the physical or logical layout of how nodes (devices) are connected to each other.

A. Star Topology

Every node connects to a central device (like a Hub or Switch) which acts as a server. Devices do not connect directly to one another.

Advantages:

  • Easy to install and manage.
  • If one cable fails, only that node goes down (easy fault isolation).

Disadvantages:

  • Central hub is a single point of failure. Entire network collapses if it dies.

B. Mesh Topology

Every node has a dedicated point-to-point link to every other node. A fully connected mesh has n(n-1)/2 physical channels.

Advantages:

  • Ultimate reliability and fault tolerance. No traffic congestion.

Disadvantages:

  • Extremely expensive and complex to wire.

C. Ring Topology

Nodes are connected in a closed-loop circle. Data travels in one direction. A "Token" is passed to prevent collisions.

Advantages:

  • Very organized; performs well under heavy traffic loads.

Disadvantages:

  • A single cable break breaks the entire ring.

D. Bus Topology

Every computer and device is connected to a single main cable (the backbone). It transmits data from one end to another.

Advantages:

  • Cost-effective and requires the least amount of cable.

Disadvantages:

  • If the main backbone cable fails, the whole network goes down.

E. Tree Topology

It has a root node and all other nodes are connected to it, forming a hierarchy (combining Bus and Star). It should have at least 3 levels.

Advantages:

  • Highly scalable. Easier fault identification.

Disadvantages:

  • If the backbone or a parent node fails, the entire sub-network is crippled.

F. Hybrid Topology

Uses a combination of two or more topologies resulting in a unique structure (e.g., star-ring or star-bus network).

Advantages:

  • Extremely flexible and very reliable.

Disadvantages:

  • Most expensive and complex to design/maintain.

5. Overview of Switching and Routing

Types of Switching

In large networks, there are multiple paths linking a sender and a receiver. Information must be "switched" as it travels through various communication channels.

1. Circuit-Switched Networks

Directly connects the sender and receiver with a dedicated physical path. It involves three phases: Setup, Data Transfer, and Teardown. While perfect for voice (like PSTN) due to zero routing delay once connected, it is highly inefficient for computer data because bandwidth is wasted when the line is idle.

2. Packet-Switched Networks

Data is broken into smaller packets. Paths are shared among many users, maximizing efficiency. Types:

  • Datagram Networks: No setup phase. Each packet is independent, carries the full destination address, and can take different routes. They may arrive out of order (The Internet).
  • Virtual-Circuit Networks: Uses a setup phase to establish a logical path and assigns a Virtual-Circuit Identifier (VCI). All packets follow the exact same path using the VCI.

3. Message-Switched Networks

An older technique using a "Store and Forward" mechanism. The entire message is sent to a node, stored completely, and then forwarded when the next link is free. Unsuitable for real-time traffic.

Overview of Routing

While switching is the mechanism of moving data from an input to an output, Routing is the intelligence behind choosing which specific path or output port to use across a massive network.

  • Path Determination: Routers use algorithms to evaluate paths based on metrics like distance (hop count), speed (bandwidth), and current congestion to find the fastest route.
  • Routing Tables: In a Datagram Network (Internet), every router contains a dynamic Routing Table. When a packet arrives, the router examines its Destination IP, compares it against the table, and forwards it.
  • Dynamic Updates: Because network conditions change constantly (cables break, traffic spikes), routers constantly communicate with each other using routing protocols (like OSPF or BGP) to update their tables dynamically.
