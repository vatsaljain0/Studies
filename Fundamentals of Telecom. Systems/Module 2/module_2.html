FTS Module 2: Basics of Signals and Transmission

1. What is a Signal? & Key Characteristics

Definition: A signal is a physical quantity (like voltage, current, or electromagnetic waves) that varies over time or space to carry information. It represents data such as speech, video, or text, allowing it to be transmitted from a source to a destination.

Examples of Signals:

  • Human voice and sound waves.
  • Voltage variations in electrical circuits.
  • Room temperature controlled by a thermostat system.
  • Electromagnetic waves used in wireless computer networks.
  • Digital music recordings and digital photographs.

Key Characteristics of a Signal:

To fully understand signals (especially periodic analog signals like sine waves), we must define their three fundamental properties:

  • Amplitude (A): The absolute value of the highest intensity (peak) of a signal, proportional to the energy it carries. Measured in Volts (V), Amperes (A), or Watts (W).
  • Frequency (f) and Period (T):
    • Period (T): The amount of time (in seconds) a signal needs to complete 1 full cycle.
    • Frequency (f): The number of cycles completed in one second. Measured in Hertz (Hz).
    • Relationship: They are inversely proportional: f = 1/T and T = 1/f.
  • Phase (φ): Describes the position of the waveform relative to time zero. It indicates how far the signal is shifted forward or backward along the time axis. Measured in degrees or radians.
  • Wavelength (λ): The physical distance a simple signal travels in one period. It ties frequency and the propagation speed of the medium together: λ = c / f (where c is the speed of light).

2. Analog vs. Digital Signals

Data can be represented either as analog (continuous) or digital (discrete) signals. The choice of signal drastically impacts transmission quality, hardware requirements, and noise immunity.

Figure 1: Smooth, continuous Sine Wave (Analog) vs. Discrete Square Wave (Digital)

Detailed Comparison

Feature Analog Signal Digital Signal
Nature Continuous and smooth varying over time. Discrete (non-continuous), jumping between specific levels.
Values Infinite number of values within a given range. Finite number of values (typically two: 0 and 1).
Waveform Type Sine wave. Square wave.
Noise Immunity Highly susceptible to noise. Degradation heavily alters the original data. Highly immune to noise. Minor voltage shifts don't flip a 0 to a 1 easily.
Bandwidth Consumes less bandwidth. Requires higher bandwidth to transmit the sharp edges of square waves.
Error Correction Difficult or impossible to perfectly regenerate original signal if corrupted. Easy to detect and correct errors using checksums and parity bits.
Examples Human voice, traditional thermometers, AM/FM radio, analog clocks. Computers, optical drives, digital clocks, digital streaming.

3. Bandwidth, Frequency Spectrum, and The EM Spectrum

  • Frequency Spectrum: A real-world signal is almost never a single perfect sine wave; it is a composite of many different frequencies. The frequency spectrum is the entire collection of all individual frequencies present in a specific signal.
  • Bandwidth: The width of the frequency spectrum. It is the absolute difference between the highest and lowest frequencies contained in the signal.
    Bandwidth = Highest Frequency (fmax) - Lowest Frequency (fmin)
    Significance: A higher bandwidth acts like a wider highway, allowing vastly more data or information to be transmitted in a given amount of time (which translates to higher data speeds in networks).

The Electromagnetic (EM) Spectrum

When electrons move, they create electromagnetic waves that can propagate through space. The entire range of these frequencies is the EM Spectrum. Different frequency bands behave differently and are used for different telecommunication purposes:

Band Approx. Frequency Range Characteristics & Telecom Use Cases
Radio Waves 3 kHz – 1 GHz Omnidirectional. Easily penetrates walls. Used in AM/FM radio, Maritime communication, standard Wi-Fi.
Microwaves 1 GHz – 300 GHz Unidirectional (requires Line-of-Sight). Cannot penetrate solid objects well. Used in Cellular networks (4G/5G), Radar, Satellite links.
Infrared 300 GHz – 400 THz Cannot penetrate walls (good for security). Very short range. Used in TV remotes, short-range wireless mice.
Visible Light 400 THz – 900 THz Used in Fiber Optic cables via lasers/LEDs to transmit massive amounts of digital data globally.
UV / X-Ray / Gamma > 900 THz Harmful to humans; very difficult to produce/modulate safely. Not used for standard telecommunications.

4. Transmission Impairments

As a signal travels through a medium, it is never perfect. The signal received at the destination is always slightly different from the one sent. This degradation is caused by three major impairments:

  • 1. Attenuation (Energy Loss): As a signal travels, it loses energy to overcome the resistance of the medium. The amplitude of the signal decreases.
    Solution: We use Amplifiers (for analog) or Repeaters (for digital) at specific intervals to boost the signal strength back up.
  • 2. Distortion (Shape Alteration): Occurs primarily in composite signals (signals made of multiple frequencies). Because different frequencies travel at slightly different speeds through a wire, they arrive at the receiver at different times, changing the overall shape of the wave.
    Solution: Equalizers are used to delay faster frequencies so all frequencies arrive together.
  • 3. Noise: Unwanted external energy that corrupts the signal. This is the biggest enemy of data transmission.
    • Thermal Noise: Random motion of electrons in the wire creating extra signals.
    • Induced Noise: From external sources like motors or appliances acting as transmitting antennas.
    • Crosstalk: The effect of one wire's magnetic field bleeding into an adjacent wire (e.g., hearing someone else's conversation on a phone line).
    • Impulse Noise: Sudden high-energy spikes (like lightning or a power surge) that can destroy data.

5. Transmission Media: Guided vs. Unguided

Transmission media is the physical path between the transmitter and the receiver. It is broadly classified into two categories: Guided (Wired) and Unguided (Wireless).

Comparison: Guided vs Unguided Media

Basis Guided (Wired) Media Unguided (Wireless) Media
Signal Direction Signals are directed and confined within a solid physical path. Signals are broadcasted through the air in multiple directions.
Security Highly secure. Difficult to tap into the physical wire. Less secure. Anyone with an antenna in range can intercept.
Installation Requires physical laying of cables (difficult over difficult terrain). Easier to deploy across difficult terrains (e.g., mountains).
Attenuation Lower attenuation (signals travel further without massive loss). High attenuation (signals degrade quickly in open air/rain).

A. Guided (Wired) Media Breakdown

1. Twisted Pair Cable

Consists of two independently insulated copper wires twisted around each other. The twisting is crucial: it helps cancel out electromagnetic interference (crosstalk) from adjacent pairs and external sources.

  • Unshielded Twisted Pair (UTP): The most common type. Very cheap, easy to install. Used heavily in local area networks (LANs) and telephones. Susceptible to external interference.
  • Shielded Twisted Pair (STP): Has an extra metal foil wrapper around the pairs to block electromagnetic interference. Used in industrial environments. More expensive and thicker.

2. Coaxial Cable

Consists of an inner solid copper core, surrounded by an insulating layer, covered by a braided metal mesh (shielding), and finally an outer plastic jacket. It carries much higher frequency signals than twisted pair due to its superior shielding.

  • Used historically in Cable TV networks, broadband internet drops, and CCTV systems.
  • More resistant to interference than UTP but bulkier to handle.

3. Fiber-Optic Cable

Transmits data not as electrical signals, but as light pulses. It uses the physical principle of Total Internal Reflection.

  • Components: An ultra-thin glass Core, surrounded by a Cladding (glass/plastic with lower refractive index to trap light inside), and an outer protective Jacket.
  • Advantages: Extremely high bandwidth, zero electromagnetic interference (completely immune to noise/crosstalk), excellent for long-distance communication (internet backbones).
  • Disadvantages: High cost, fragile (requires careful installation), and difficult to splice/connect. Unidirectional (needs two fibers for 2-way communication).

B. Unguided (Wireless) Media Breakdown

Wireless media carry electromagnetic waves without using a physical conductor. They broadcast signals through the air (or vacuum).

Feature Radio Waves Microwaves Infrared Waves
Frequency Range 3 kHz – 1 GHz 1 GHz – 300 GHz 300 GHz – 400 THz
Directionality Omnidirectional (propagates in all directions). Antenna does not need alignment. Unidirectional (moves in a focused single line). Antennas must be strictly aligned. Highly directional / Unidirectional.
Wall Penetration Can easily penetrate solid objects (walls, buildings). Cannot penetrate solid objects well (blocked by walls, hills, rain). Cannot penetrate solid objects at all.
Special Requirements Regulated heavily by governments to avoid interference. Requires strict Line-of-Sight (antennas must face each other without obstruction). Requires direct line-of-sight over very short ranges. Prevents interference between rooms.
Applications AM/FM Radio, TV broadcasting, standard Wi-Fi, maritime comms. Point-to-point links, Radar, Satellite communication, Cellular networks. TV Remote controls, Bluetooth, Wireless mice/keyboards, Mobile-to-printer.
