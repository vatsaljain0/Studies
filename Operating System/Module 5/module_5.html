Digital Communication Techniques

Detailed Exam Edition: Deep Concepts, Plain English, Formulas & Diagrams

1. Fundamentals of Communication Systems

The 5 Blocks of a Communication System

For a long-answer question, draw 5 sequential boxes and explain the role and distortion of each block:

  1. Information Source: Produces the raw message (like human voice, video, or computer data).
  2. Transmitter: Processes the raw message. It performs Encoding (converting data to a standard format) and Modulation (attaching it to a carrier wave). This stage can introduce "Transmitter Distortion."
  3. Channel: The physical transmission medium (copper wire, fiber optics, or open air). As the signal travels, external Noise Sources (like electrical interference or weather) attack and degrade the signal.
  4. Receiver: Captures the signal from the channel. It performs Decoding and Demodulation to extract the original message. This stage can introduce "Receiver Distortion."
  5. Destination: The final user or machine that receives and understands the message.

Hardware Components of a Radio Transmitter

If asked to explain a Radio Transmitter block diagram, detail these exact components:

  • Crystal Oscillator: The "heartbeat" of the transmitter. It generates a perfectly steady, pure, high-frequency base wave (the Carrier Wave).
  • RF Buffer Amplifier: Isolates the oscillator from the rest of the heavy circuitry. This prevents the high frequencies from drifting or becoming unstable.
  • Modulation Processing & Modulator: Takes your actual voice (the input signal) and mixes it into the carrier wave generated by the oscillator.
  • RF Output Power Amplifier: Takes the mixed signal and gives it a massive surge of electrical power so the antenna can physically radiate it miles into the air.

2. Modulation Techniques (AM, FM, PM)

What is Modulation?

Simple Definition: Modulation is like attaching a light paper letter (your voice) to a heavy rock (the carrier wave) so you can throw it far. Your voice is too weak to travel miles through the air. So, we "mix" it with a very powerful, high-frequency radio wave that carries it to the destination.

Why do we need it?

  • Antenna Size: An antenna must be proportional to the signal's size. Low frequencies (like human voice) would need an antenna miles long! High-frequency carrier waves let us use tiny antennas (like on a phone).
  • Wireless Range: Low frequencies die out quickly. Modulated high frequencies travel massive distances.

A. Amplitude Modulation (AM)

1. Definition:

AM is a technique where we change the height (amplitude) of the powerful carrier wave to match the exact shape of your voice signal.

2. How it works:

When your voice gets louder, the carrier wave grows taller. When your voice gets softer, the carrier wave shrinks. The receiver simply traces the outline (height) of the wave to get your voice back.

3. Diagram:

AM Wave: Notice the height changing, but the waves come at the same speed.

4. Dependence:

  • What Changes: The Amplitude (Height).
  • What Stays Constant: Frequency (Speed) and Phase (Starting Angle).
  • Depends on: The height changes based strictly on the loudness (voltage) of your voice signal.

5. Uses & Disadvantages:

  • Uses: AM Radio stations. Travels incredibly far distances because it can bounce off the Earth's atmosphere.
  • Disadvantages: Awful sound quality. Lightning and machines naturally change the height of waves in the air. Since AM relies on height to store data, this static noise ruins the sound.

6. Numerical (How to solve easily):

Modulation Index (m): A score from 0 to 1 that tells you how deep the modulation is. If it goes over 1, the wave breaks and sound is distorted.
Formula: m = (Max Voltage - Min Voltage) / (Max Voltage + Min Voltage)

Example: Max voltage is 10V, Min is 2V.
m = (10 - 2) / (10 + 2) = 8 / 12 = 0.66 (Good! Less than 1).

7. Any Other Concept:

Bandwidth: AM creates two extra waves on the sides (Sidebands). So the total size needed is always exactly 2 × Message Frequency.

B. Frequency Modulation (FM)

1. Definition:

FM is a technique where we change the speed (frequency) of the carrier wave to match the voice signal. The height stays perfectly flat.

2. How it works:

When your voice gets louder, the carrier waves squish tightly together (speed up). When your voice gets softer, the waves stretch far apart (slow down). The receiver reads how fast the waves hit it to get your voice back.

3. Diagram:

FM Wave: Notice the height is flat, but the waves squish and stretch.

4. Dependence:

  • What Changes: The Frequency (Speed).
  • What Stays Constant: Amplitude (Height) and Phase.
  • Depends on: How much the frequency squishes depends strictly on the loudness (amplitude) of the voice signal.

5. Uses & Disadvantages:

  • Uses: FM Radio and TV audio. Excellent sound quality because it ignores static noise (since static only affects height, and FM doesn't care about height).
  • Disadvantages: Circuit is expensive and complex to build. Cannot travel as far as AM (blocked by buildings easily).

6. Numerical (How to solve easily):

The Problem: Given an FM equation like v = 12 sin(600 t + 5 cos 125 t). Find the values.

The Trick: Just match it to the Master Formula:

v = [Voltage] sin( [Carrier Speed] t + [Index] cos [Message Speed] t )

  • Voltage: The first number = 12 Volts.
  • Carrier Frequency (Fc): Take the Carrier Speed and divide by 6.28 (2π). So, 600 / 6.28 = 95.5 Hz.
  • Message Frequency (Fm): Take the Message Speed and divide by 6.28 (2π). So, 125 / 6.28 = 19.9 Hz.
  • Modulation Index (m): The number right before 'cos' = 5.
  • Max Deviation: Simply multiply Index × Message Freq (5 × 19.9 = 99.5 Hz).

7. Any Other Concept:

Wideband vs Narrowband: Wideband FM (WBFM) allows huge frequency squishing for super-high-quality music, but takes up a lot of space. Narrowband FM (NBFM) only allows tiny squishing, saving space, used in walkie-talkies.

C. Phase Modulation (PM)

1. Definition:

PM is a technique where we change the starting angle (phase) of the carrier wave based on the voice signal. Like FM, the height stays completely flat.

2. How it works:

Instead of making the wave go faster or slower, PM suddenly shifts the wave forward or backward in time. Imagine a swinging pendulum that suddenly gets pushed forward a bit; that "push" represents your voice data.

3. Diagram:

PM Wave: Notice the sharp, sudden breaks (shifts) where the wave resets its cycle.

4. Dependence:

  • What Changes: The Phase (Starting Angle).
  • What Stays Constant: Amplitude and Frequency.
  • Depends on: The phase shifts based strictly on the amplitude (loudness) of the message. Unlike FM, it doesn't care at all about the message frequency.

5. Uses & Disadvantages:

  • Uses: The absolute foundation for modern digital networks like Wi-Fi and 5G (using Phase-Shift Keying). Highly secure and efficient for computer data.
  • Disadvantages: Incredibly complex and expensive hardware required to decode the sudden phase shifts. Not used for basic analog radio.

6. Numerical (How to solve easily):

In Phase Modulation, the math is delightfully simple! The Modulation Index is exactly equal to the Maximum Phase Deviation. If they tell you the wave deviates by 5 radians, then the index is just 5. Done!

7. Any Other Concept:

Phase modulation is almost identical to Frequency modulation mathematically. They are brothers. If you change the phase, the frequency briefly changes too! But the core difference is PM only reacts to the height of the message, while FM reacts to both height and speed.

3. Multiplexing (Combining Signals)

What is Multiplexing?

Simple Definition: Multiplexing is the technique of combining many different signals into one massive signal so they can all travel through a single shared cable or airway at the same time.

Imagine a highway. Instead of building 100 different tiny roads for 100 cars, we build one massive highway and let all the cars share it. A Multiplexer (MUX) merges the signals at the start, and a Demultiplexer (DEMUX) separates them back to normal at the end, saving massive amounts of money on cables.

The Different Types of Multiplexing

There are four main ways to share a communication channel. Think of them simply like this:

  • FDM (Frequency Division): Splitting the channel into different "Frequency Lanes". Everyone gets their own lane to drive in continuously.
  • TDM (Time Division): Everyone uses the exact same lane, but they take turns really fast.
  • WDM (Wavelength Division): Used for fiber optics. Sending different signals using different colors of laser light at the same time.
  • CDMA (Code Division): Everyone transmits at the exact same time and frequency, but speaks a different "coded language" so the receiver can filter them out.

A. Frequency Division Multiplexing (FDM)

What it is & How it works:

FDM is used for Analog signals. It takes the total bandwidth (the size of the cable) and chops it up into smaller, non-overlapping "slots". Each user is assigned their own specific slot. Because they are in different slots, they can all transmit continuously at the exact same time without crashing into each other.

Professional Working (How the Telecom Industry does it):

In real-world telephone networks, millions of calls need to be managed. Engineers bundle them into standardized groups to keep things organized:

  • The Basic Group: The smallest building block. Engineers take exactly 12 telephone channels (each given 4 kHz of space) and bundle them together into one group spanning 60 to 108 kHz.
  • The Pilot Signal: To make sure the equipment stays perfectly tuned and synchronized, a continuous low-level "pilot" tone is injected at exactly 104.08 kHz.
  • The Basic Supergroup: To scale up, they take 5 Basic Groups (60 total calls) and bundle them into an even larger supergroup.

Apply it to an Example:

Radio Stations: The air around you is the shared cable. Radio stations use FDM! One station broadcasts exactly at 98.3 MHz, and another broadcasts at 104.2 MHz. They are both in the air at the exact same time, but because they are in different frequency "slots", your radio can easily separate them. (Note: They leave small blank spaces between stations called "Guard Bands" so the signals don't accidentally bleed into each other).

B. Time Division Multiplexing (TDM)

What it is & How it works:

TDM is used for Digital signals. Instead of splitting the channel into frequency slots, TDM makes everyone use the exact same full frequency! However, it separates users by Time. The system rapidly takes turns, giving User 1 the cable for a microsecond, then User 2, then User 3, in a continuous circle. Because digital pulses are very fast, you don't even notice the gaps.

Professional Working (The Hardware Mechanism):

How does a machine actually take turns so fast? It uses a standard 24-channel system built with high-speed electronic switches and logic gates:

  • Sampling & Framing: The system takes 8,000 snapshots of a voice every second. It packages one snapshot from all 24 users into a "Frame" and transmits the entire frame in just 125 microseconds.
  • Transmitter (Delay Lines): To line the users up perfectly, the hardware uses Delay Lines. User 1's signal goes straight through. User 2's signal is delayed by exactly 5 microseconds so it falls perfectly in line right behind User 1. User 3 is delayed by 10 microseconds, and so on.
  • Receiver (AND Gates): The receiver uses 24 AND gates (digital doors). A synchronized clock sends a tiny trigger pulse to open the doors. Delay lines are used on the clock so that Door #2 only opens at the exact microsecond User 2's data arrives.

Apply it to an Example:

Taking Turns on a Phone Line: Imagine 24 people trying to talk through one single telephone wire. In TDM, the system uses digital switching hardware (like logic gates) to take a tiny 1-millisecond snippet of Person 1's voice and sends it. Then a 1-millisecond snippet of Person 2's voice. It repeats this circle 8,000 times a second! The receiver on the other side catches the snippets and glues them back together smoothly.

Difference Between FDM and TDM

Feature FDM (Frequency Division) TDM (Time Division)
How it shares Shares by dividing Frequencies. Shares by dividing Time.
Signal Type Used primarily for Analog signals. Used primarily for Digital signals.
Time vs Bandwidth User transmits all the time, but only gets a tiny piece of the bandwidth. User gets the full bandwidth, but only for a tiny piece of time.
Interference Problem Suffers from Crosstalk (channels bleeding into each other). Needs guard bands. Suffers from Synchronization issues. If clocks are off, data mixes up. Needs sync bits.

4. Error Detection, Correction & Matrices

During transmission, noise and system impairments can corrupt the data stream (e.g., flipping a 1 to a 0). Procedures must be implemented to detect and fix these errors, though doing so increases transmission costs.

A. Error Detection Methods

  • Parity-Check Codes: The most popular form of redundancy. A parity bit (a 1 or 0) is added to the end of a character block to force the total sum of 1s to be either exclusively odd or exclusively even. If the receiver calculates the wrong parity, it requests a retransmission.
  • Constant-Ratio Codes: Provides inherent error detection. For example, the 2-out-of-5 code enforces a strict pattern: every received block must contain exactly two 1s and three 0s. If the ratio alters, an error is flagged.
    The Limitation: An odd number of errors will be detected, but an even number of errors (one 1 flips to 0, and one 0 flips to 1 simultaneously) will maintain the ratio and sneak past undetected.
    Combinatorics Formula: Number of valid combinations = T! / [ M! × (T - M)! ] (Where T is Total bits, M is number of 1s, and ! is Factorial).
  • Redundant Codes (100% Replication): Transmitting the exact same information twice and comparing the two sets. Statistically, it is unlikely a random error will hit the exact same spot twice.
    Formula: Efficiency = Information Bits / Total Bits. In complete retransmission, efficiency is an abysmal 50%.

B. Error Correction Methods

1. Retransmission (ARQ)

The most popular and expeditious method. Uses an Automatic Request for Repeat (ARQ) or ACK/NAK system.

If parity is correct upon receipt, a positive acknowledgment (ACK) is sent, and the next block begins. If a parity error is detected, a negative acknowledgment (NAK) is sent, and the transmitter repeats just that specific block.

2. Forward Error-Correcting (FEC) Codes

For maximum transmission efficiency over difficult links, we want to correct errors without waiting for retransmissions. FEC codes include heavy mathematical redundancy in the transmission, allowing the receiver to independently find and fix the corrupted bits.

FEC Exam Example: The 3-Level Matrix Sum

A powerful FEC method is the matrix sum, where data is transmitted in a grid with checksums attached to the end of every row and column. (The sum of the rows must equal the sum of the columns).

1. Normal Encoding & Sending:

Suppose the message is "ADD". We convert to alphabet numbers: A=1, D=4, D=4.
Row 1 is: [1, 4, 4]. The checksum total is 9. The 9th letter is 'I'. We transmit the string "ADDI".

2. Catching and Fixing an Error (Step-by-Step Logic):

Imagine electrical noise hits the very first number during transmission, corrupting the '1' into an 11. The receiver constructs the matrix:

  • Row 1 Received as: [11, 4, 4].
    Internal sum is 19. But the received Checksum 'I' dictates the sum must be 9! (Error flagged in Row 1).
  • Column 1 Received as: [11, 2].
    Internal sum is 13. But the received Column Checksum dictates it must be 3! (Error flagged in Column 1).

The Correction: Since Row 1 and Column 1 both failed their checksums, the corrupted bit MUST be at their intersection (the 11).

Look at the mathematical variance: The row total is 19, but it should be 9 (Variance of +10). The column total is 13, but it should be 3 (Variance of +10).

We simply take the corrupted number (11), subtract the exact variance (10), resulting in 1. The data is mathematically restored to its correct original form without requiring any ARQ retransmission!
